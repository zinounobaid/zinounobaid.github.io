Overview

The goal of this project was to reduce the mass of a lever-like aircraft component while maintaining structural integrity under three critical load cases. Using CAD and FEA in ANSYS, I iteratively removed material from low-stress regions, validating each design against stress and deflection limits.

Problem

Aircraft components must stay within allowable stress and deflection limits while being as light as possible to improve fuel efficiency. The starting design met the structural requirements but was heavier than necessary. The task was to reduce weight without exceeding allowable stress or deflection under:

  • 1,250 lb horizontal load at the eye of the lever
  • 1,875 lb load at 45° (combined horizontal + vertical)
  • 2,500 lb vertical load at the eye

Approach

I imported the baseline CAD into ANSYS and defined realistic boundary conditions by fixing only the bolt-hole contact regions instead of the entire bottom face, since that area was a candidate for future material removal.

  • Ran initial analyses with a normal mesh to identify low-stress regions that were good candidates for material removal.
  • Iteratively removed material in SolidWorks from consistently low-stress regions, exported the updated geometry, and re-ran FEA for all three load cases.
  • Refined the mesh using a global body sizing of 0.1 in and checked mesh quality plots to ensure the optimized design was still well-resolved.
  • After each iteration, compared maximum Von Mises stress and deflection against allowable limits and reverted or adjusted cuts if stresses increased too much.

Results

  • Reduced part mass from 110.86 g to 48.51 g, a weight reduction of approximately 56%.
  • Maintained a maximum Von Mises stress below material yield, with an overall factor of safety of about 1.03 (145 ksi allowable vs. ~140 ksi max).
  • Kept deflection within the original allowable limits for all three load cases.
  • Achieved high mesh quality in the final design, with only a small region of lower-quality elements localized near the applied load.

Notes & Future Improvements

Future work could include applying topology optimization to generate an even more efficient material layout, running fatigue and thermal analyses for additional load cases, and comparing manufacturability and cost across different design variants and processes.
